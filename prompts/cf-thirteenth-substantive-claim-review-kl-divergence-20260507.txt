Claim under review:
"KL divergence is a directional expectation under the first distribution of a log probability ratio; it is nonnegative, generally asymmetric, and differs from cross-entropy by the fixed entropy of the target distribution."

Cited source:
Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville, "Deep Learning", MIT Press (2016), Chapter 3 Probability and Information Theory, https://www.deeplearningbook.org/contents/prob.html

Primary-source evidence I checked before this prompt from the official Deep Learning Book Chapter 3 HTML:
- Chapter 3, around book page 71: Goodfellow et al. define Shannon entropy as an expectation under P.
- Chapter 3, around book page 71: They define KL divergence for two distributions P(x) and Q(x) over the same random variable x as D_KL(P || Q) = E_{x~P}[log(P(x)/Q(x))] = E_{x~P}[log P(x) - log Q(x)].
- Chapter 3, around book page 71: For discrete variables, they describe KL as the extra information needed when symbols are drawn from P but encoded using a code designed for Q.
- Chapter 3, around book pages 71-72: They state KL has useful properties, most notably being non-negative, and is 0 iff P and Q are the same distribution in the discrete case, or equal almost everywhere in the continuous case.
- Chapter 3, around book pages 71-72: They state KL is not a true distance because it is not symmetric, and that the direction D_KL(P || Q) versus D_KL(Q || P) has important consequences.
- Chapter 3, around book page 72: They define cross-entropy as H(P,Q)=H(P)+D_KL(P||Q), equivalently H(P,Q)=-E_{x~P} log Q(x), and say minimizing cross-entropy with respect to Q is equivalent to minimizing KL because Q does not participate in the omitted term.
- Chapter 3, around book page 72: They state the information-theory convention for terms like 0 log 0.
