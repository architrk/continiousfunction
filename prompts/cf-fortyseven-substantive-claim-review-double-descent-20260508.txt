Primary source evidence gathered for this deeper review:

Belkin et al. 2018/2019, "Reconciling modern machine learning practice and the bias-variance trade-off":
- arXiv abstract says the double-descent curve subsumes the U-shaped bias-variance curve and shows that increasing model capacity beyond interpolation can improve performance.
- The paper describes the double-descent risk curve as the classical U-shaped regime plus a modern interpolating regime separated by the interpolation threshold, with predictors to the right having zero training risk.
- It says learned predictors at the interpolation threshold typically have high risk, while increasing function-class capacity beyond that point leads to decreasing risk.
- It also gives empirical evidence across random features, neural networks, forests, boosting, and synthetic examples, while noting regularization, early stopping, and sampling can mask the interpolation peak.

Nakkiran et al. 2019, "Deep Double Descent: Where Bigger Models and More Data Hurt":
- arXiv abstract says modern deep-learning tasks exhibit double descent where increasing model size first worsens then improves performance.
- The same abstract says double descent also appears as a function of training epochs.
- It further says their effective-model-complexity view identifies regimes where increasing train samples can hurt test performance.
- Source text describes the transition: test error decreases, then increases around the interpolation threshold where the model/training procedure just barely reaches near-zero train error, then undergoes a second descent.
- Source text separately discusses model-wise double descent, epoch-wise double descent, and sample-size non-monotonicity / sample-wise regimes.
