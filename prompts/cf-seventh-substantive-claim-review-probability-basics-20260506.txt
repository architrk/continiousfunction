"In a finite probability model, events are subsets of a sample space whose probabilities are nonnegative, add over disjoint unions, total one on the whole space, and get renormalized when conditioning on observed evidence."

Primary source checked:

  - Chapter 6.1-6.3, Construction of a Probability Space / Discrete and Continuous Probabilities / Sum Rule, Product Rule, and Bayes' Theorem.

Source evidence I checked:
- MML chapter 6.1 says a probability space models random outcomes and introduces sample space, event space, and probability measure.
- It defines the sample space `Omega` as the set of all possible outcomes.
- It says a subset `A` of the sample space is in the event space if we can observe whether an outcome is in `A`; for discrete probability distributions, the event space is often the power set of `Omega`.
- It says each event `A` gets a probability `P(A)`; the probability of a single event lies in `[0,1]`, and the total probability over all outcomes is `P(Omega)=1`.
- In its finite coin example, it computes `P(($, £) union (£, $)) = P(($, £)) + P((£, $))`, using additivity over distinct/disjoint outcomes.
- MML section 6.2 defines conditional probabilities in a finite probability table as fractions such as `P(Y=y_j | X=x_i)=n_ij/c_i` and `P(X=x_i | Y=y_j)=n_ij/r_j`, which is exactly row/column renormalization.
- MML section 6.3 gives the product rule, derives Bayes' theorem, and says the evidence/marginal likelihood normalizes the posterior.
