Exact claim:
"Cross-entropy measures target-weighted surprise: the target distribution supplies averaging weights, the model distribution supplies scored probabilities, and for one-hot labels it becomes the negative log probability of the correct class."

Primary source checked:
- Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville, "Deep Learning", official HTML book, https://www.deeplearningbook.org/
  - Chapter 3 Probability and Information Theory: https://www.deeplearningbook.org/contents/prob.html
  - Chapter 5 Machine Learning Basics: https://www.deeplearningbook.org/contents/ml.html

Source evidence I checked:
- Chapter 3.13 defines self-information/surprise as `I(x) = -log P(x)` and Shannon entropy as the expected information under `x ~ P`.
- Chapter 3.13 defines `D_KL(P||Q) = E_{x~P}[log P(x) - log Q(x)]`, so the direction and averaging distribution are explicitly `P`.
- Chapter 3.13 relates KL to cross-entropy through `H(P,Q)=H(P)+D_KL(P||Q)`.
- Chapter 5.5 derives maximum likelihood as maximizing the empirical expectation of `log p_model(x; theta)`, equivalently minimizing `-E_{x~p_data} log p_model(x)`.
- Chapter 5.5 says minimizing KL from the empirical/data distribution to the model is exactly minimizing cross-entropy between those distributions.
- Chapter 5.5 says any negative log-likelihood consisting of a cross-entropy between an empirical distribution and a model distribution is commonly called cross-entropy, and specifically mentions negative log-likelihood of Bernoulli or softmax distributions.

Local witness alignment:
- The page states `H(p,q) = -sum_k p_k log q_k` and `H(p,q) = E_{X~p}[-log q_X]`.
- The page states the direction: `p` supplies averaging weights and `q` supplies the probabilities being scored.
- The page states the one-hot case: if `p_y = 1` and all other `p_k = 0`, then `H(p,q) = -log q_y`.
- The page's caveat limits this review to categorical cross-entropy as a probabilistic loss, not multi-label sigmoid BCE, calibration, or classifier correctness.
