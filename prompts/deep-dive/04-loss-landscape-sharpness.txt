### Concept: Loss Landscapes, Sharpness & Flat Minima (SAM)

**Core math:**
```
SAM objective: min_w max_{|ε|_p ≤ ρ} L(w + ε)

Practical SAM:
1. ε(w) ≈ ρ * ∇L(w) / |∇L(w)|₂  (worst-case perturbation)
2. Update using ∇L(w + ε(w))    (gradient at perturbed point)

Sharpness ≈ max eigenvalue of Hessian ∇²L(w)
```

**Missing intuition to address:**
- Sharp vs flat minima in 2D looks simple, but high-dimensional anisotropic sharpness is complex
- Flat minima → better generalization (more robust to perturbations)
- Mode connectivity: many minima connected by low-loss paths
- Why SGD implicitly finds flatter minima than full-batch GD

**Interactive visualization:**

1. **2D Loss Surface with Two Minima:**
   - One sharp minimum (high curvature, deep)
   - One flat minimum (low curvature, slightly higher loss)
   - Contour plot with Hessian eigenvalue visualization

2. **Perturbation Circle:**
   - Show the ρ-ball around current weights
   - Animate the "worst-case perturbation" direction
   - Compare loss at w vs max loss in ball

3. **SAM vs SGD Trajectory:**
   - Toggle between standard SGD and SAM updates
   - Show how SAM "seeks" flatter regions
   - Display sharpness metric along trajectory

4. **Sliders:**
   - Perturbation radius ρ (0.01 to 0.5)
   - Learning rate
   - Toggle: SGD vs SAM

5. **Hessian Eigenvalue Display:**
   - Show λ_max (sharpness) at current point
   - Visualize eigenvector direction (direction of max curvature)
   - Color-code regions by sharpness

**Key insights to convey:**
- Training loss alone doesn't predict generalization
- Flat minima are more "compressible" / lower description length
- SAM explicitly optimizes for flatness by looking at worst-case nearby loss
- Mini-batch noise in SGD implicitly regularizes toward flat minima

**Educational panels:**
