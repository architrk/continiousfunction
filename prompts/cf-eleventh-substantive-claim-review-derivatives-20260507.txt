Claim under review:
"For a differentiable scalar function, the derivative at x is the limit of the difference quotient as h approaches 0: secant slopes over [x,x+h] converge to the tangent slope, a local one-variable rate-of-change signal used by gradient-based optimization."

Cited source:
Deisenroth, Faisal, and Ong, "Mathematics for Machine Learning", Cambridge University Press, 2020, https://mml-book.github.io/

Primary-source evidence I checked before this prompt from the official MML PDF available on the companion site:
- PDF page 146 / book page 140: MML says it will discuss gradients of functions, which are often essential for learning in machine learning models because the gradient points in the direction of steepest ascent.
- PDF page 147 / book page 141: MML section 5.1 starts with the difference quotient for a univariate function y=f(x), x,y in R, and defines it as [f(x+delta x)-f(x)]/delta x.
- PDF page 147 / book page 141: MML states the difference quotient computes the slope of the secant line through two points on the graph, can be considered an average slope between x and x+delta x, and in the limit delta x -> 0 gives the tangent at x if f is differentiable.
- PDF page 147 / book page 141: Definition 5.2 defines the derivative for h>0 as df/dx := lim_{h->0} [f(x+h)-f(x)]/h and says the secant becomes a tangent; it also states the derivative points in the direction of steepest ascent.
- PDF page 234 / book page 228: MML section 7.1 defines gradient descent for differentiable f: R^d -> R, recalls from section 5.1 that the gradient points in the direction of steepest ascent, and says gradient descent moves in the negative gradient direction for steepest descent.
- PDF page 234 / book page 228: MML gives the update x_{i+1}=x_i - gamma_i ((nabla f)(x_i))^T and frames the gradient as the local signal used by the optimization step.
