Exact claim:
"Gradient descent uses the gradient as the direction of steepest local increase and updates parameters in the negative-gradient direction, with the learning rate controlling how far to trust the local approximation."

Primary sources checked:
- Boyd and Vandenberghe, "Convex Optimization", official book page/PDF: https://web.stanford.edu/~boyd/cvxbook/
- Goodfellow, Bengio, and Courville, "Deep Learning", official HTML book:
  - Chapter 4 Numerical Computation: https://www.deeplearningbook.org/contents/numerical.html
  - Chapter 8 Optimization for Training Deep Models: https://www.deeplearningbook.org/contents/optimization.html

Source evidence I checked:
- Goodfellow chapter 4.3 introduces optimization as minimizing a cost/loss/objective, then explains derivative/gradient-based descent. It says the directional derivative in direction `u` is `u^T grad_x f(x)`, that the direction minimizing that is the negative gradient, that the gradient points directly uphill, and that gradient descent proposes `x' = x - eps grad_x f(x)` where `eps` is a positive scalar learning rate.
- Goodfellow chapter 4.3 also uses a second-order Taylor approximation around the current point to show the gradient step has an expected slope-improvement term plus a curvature correction, so a too-large step can move uphill.
- Goodfellow chapter 8.3 frames gradient descent/SGD as following training-set or minibatch gradients downhill and says learning-rate choice matters: too large can cause oscillation/cost increase, too low makes learning slow or stuck.
- Boyd and Vandenberghe chapter 9 describes descent methods as choosing a search direction and step size, gives the gradient descent method with `delta_x = -grad f(x)` and line search for the step size, and states that for the Euclidean norm the steepest descent direction is the negative gradient and coincides with gradient descent.
