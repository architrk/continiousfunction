Claim under review:
"A random variable is a measurable map from raw outcomes to values; pushing probability through that map gives its distribution, and expectations can be computed from either raw outcomes or the induced value distribution."

Cited source:
- Deisenroth, Faisal, Ong, Mathematics for Machine Learning, chapter 6.

Source evidence I checked locally:
- MML 6.1.2 defines a probability space (Omega, A, P), introduces a target space T, and defines X: Omega -> T as a random variable.
- It says this random variable transfers probability to a more convenient, often numerical space.
- It defines preimages X^-1(S) = {omega in Omega : X(omega) in S}.
- Equation 6.8 gives PX(S) = P(X in S) = P(X^-1(S)) = P({omega in Omega : X(omega) in S}).
- It says PX, equivalently P composed with X^-1, is the law or distribution of random variable X.
- MML 6.2 discusses discrete PMFs and continuous PDFs/CDFs, and notes Borel sigma-algebra technicalities for continuous real-valued random variables.
- MML 6.4.1 defines expected values for continuous random variables by integrals over p(x), and for discrete random variables by sums over p(x).
- MML 6.4 defines mean, covariance, and variance as spread summaries via expectations.
