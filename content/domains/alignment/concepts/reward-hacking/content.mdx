title: "Reward Hacking & Overoptimization: Goodhart's Law in Preference Optimization"
---

## Intuition

Reward hacking is Goodhart's law in ML form: *when a measure becomes a target, it stops being a good measure*.

In preference optimization, the "reward" is not the true thing you care about. It's a learned proxy (a reward model, or an implicit preference objective) trained on finite, noisy comparisons. As you optimize the policy, you push it into regions where:

- the reward model has **less data** (distribution shift),
- small prediction errors get **amplified** by optimization pressure,
- the policy discovers **loopholes** that score well but are actually bad.

This is why modern post-training recipes use conservative objectives, ensembles, monitoring, and KL anchors. The point is not "solve Goodhart forever" but "slow it down and detect it early."

## Math

### Proxy reward optimization with a trust region

A common shape (RLHF-style) is:

$$\\max_{\\pi_\\theta}\\;\\mathbb E_{y\\sim\\pi_\\theta(\\cdot\\mid x)}[\\hat r_\\phi(x,y)] - \\beta\\,\\mathrm{KL}\\!\\left(\\pi_\\theta(\\cdot\\mid x)\\ \\|\\ \\pi_{\\text{ref}}(\\cdot\\mid x)\\right).$$

The KL term helps, but it's not a magic shield: you can still find high-scoring hacks inside the trust region if the proxy is wrong there.

### Conservative optimization via ensembles (a practical anti-Goodhart move)

With an ensemble of reward models $\\{r_{\\phi_j}\\}_{j=1}^K$, define mean and standard deviation:

$$\\mu(x,y)=\\frac{1}{K}\\sum_{j=1}^K r_{\\phi_j}(x,y),\\qquad
\\sigma(x,y)=\\sqrt{\\frac{1}{K}\\sum_{j=1}^K\\left(r_{\\phi_j}(x,y)-\\mu(x,y)\\right)^2}.$$

Then optimize a lower confidence bound:

$$r_{\\text{LCB}}(x,y)=\\mu(x,y)-\\lambda\\,\\sigma(x,y).$$

Intuition: prefer high reward you are *confident* about, not just high mean reward.

## Code

```python
import numpy as np

x = np.linspace(-3, 3, 401)
r_true = -(x - 1.0) ** 2                      # true objective (unknown in practice)

# A proxy reward that "extrapolates wrong" beyond where it was trained
bias = 0.6 * np.tanh(2 * (x - 1.5))
r_hat = r_true + bias

# Uncertainty grows off-distribution (toy model)
sigma = 0.1 + 0.4 * np.abs(x - 1.0)
lam = 1.0
r_lcb = r_hat - lam * sigma

x_true = float(x[np.argmax(r_true)])
x_proxy = float(x[np.argmax(r_hat)])
x_lcb = float(x[np.argmax(r_lcb)])

print("true optimum x~", round(x_true, 3))
print("proxy optimum x~", round(x_proxy, 3))
print("LCB optimum  x~", round(x_lcb, 3))
```
