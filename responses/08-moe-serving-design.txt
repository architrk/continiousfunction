## 1) Foundation Concept #23: **MoE Serving & Scheduling (Token Dispatch, All‑to‑All, Disaggregated Expert Parallelism)**

**Why this is the optimal next step (#21 → #22 → #23):**

* **Latest 2024–2025 research is *squarely* here.** The hottest MoE work lately isn’t “what is a router?”—it’s *how do you actually serve MoEs efficiently* when routing creates **skew + all‑to‑all traffic + tiny per‑expert batch sizes**. Systems like **Aurora (2024)** and **MegaScale‑Infer (2025)** exist because naïve MoE serving leaves huge performance on the table. ([arXiv][1])
* **Big educational gap:** Most explanations stop at “MoE reduces FLOPs” and miss the production reality:

  * MoE often turns the FFN path into a **memory/communication** problem, not a compute problem. ([arXiv][2])
  * Performance becomes a **straggler problem** (the busiest expert/GPU sets latency).
  * Serving becomes a **routing‑dependent queueing/scheduling** problem.
* **Natural connection to your existing map:**

  * #21 taught **prefill vs decode**, SLOs, continuous batching.
  * #22 taught **routing + load balancing + expert parallelism**.
  * #23 explains what actually happens when you combine them: **token dispatch, expert placement, batching policies, and communication scheduling** are the “hidden curriculum” of modern MoE inference. ([arXiv][2])
* **Practical frontier-model impact:** Open and frontier-grade models increasingly ship as MoEs (e.g., **Mixtral**, **DeepSeek‑V2/V3**, **DBRX**)—so if learners can’t reason about MoE *inference*, they can’t reason about modern LLM cost/latency tradeoffs. ([arXiv][3])

## 2) Math core (2–3 equations)

### (A) Sparse routing + top‑k selection

Let (h_t \in \mathbb{R}^{d}) be the token’s hidden state. The router produces expert probabilities:
[
p_t = \mathrm{softmax}(W_r h_t)\in \Delta^{E-1},
\quad S_t=\mathrm{TopK}(p_t,k),
\quad g_{t,e}=\frac{p_{t,e}}{\sum_{j\in S_t}p_{t,j}}.
]

### (B) MoE layer as “dispatch → expert compute → combine”

[
y_t=\sum_{e\in S_t} g_{t,e}, f_e(h_t).
]
This is the *model* view. #23’s key shift is to treat this as a **systems pipeline** that must physically move tokens to the devices holding those (f_e).

### (C) The “hidden tax”: dispatch/return communication volume

A crude but very useful first-order model for per-layer communication (send activations to experts + return outputs) is:
[
\text{Bytes}*{\text{MoE}} \approx 2 \cdot T \cdot k \cdot d*{\text{model}} \cdot b,
\quad
t_{\text{comm}} \approx \frac{\text{Bytes}*{\text{MoE}}}{\text{BW}*{\text{net}}}.
]
where (T) = tokens in the microbatch, (b) = bytes/element (e.g., fp16=2). This is why “sparse compute” can still be **network/memory bound** in practice. ([arXiv][2])

## 3) Frontier model connections (why this matters in real models)

* **Mixtral (2024):** each MoE layer has **8 experts**, and the router selects **2 experts per token**, combining their outputs. This is the canonical “top‑2 MoE” modern users deploy—and it *forces* token dispatch + combine every MoE layer. ([arXiv][3])
* **DeepSeek‑V2 (2024):** a large MoE with **236B total parameters** but **21B activated per token**, explicitly emphasizing economical training and efficient inference—exactly the setting where serving/scheduling decisions dominate cost. ([arXiv][4])
* **DeepSeek‑V3 (late 2024):** scales to **671B total / 37B activated**, again reinforcing that “active params” doesn’t automatically mean “easy inference”—the system must keep utilization high under routing skew. 
* **DBRX (2024):** “fine‑grained” MoE with **132B total / 36B active**—another example where real-world throughput depends on how well you handle expert dispatch and balance. ([Databricks][5])
* **What the systems papers highlight:**

  * In MoE decoding, **per-expert token counts shrink** at fixed batch size as sparsity/expert count grows → experts stop being compute-saturating GEMMs and become underutilized. ([arXiv][2])
  * **Disaggregating attention vs FFN/expert modules** can restore utilization and hide communication. ([arXiv][2])

## 4) The “missing intuition” this concept should finally explain

Most learners are missing a few mental models that make MoE *click* in production:

1. **“Sparsity buys FLOPs, but it sells you a scheduling problem.”**
   Dense FFNs are predictable; MoE FFNs are **demand-shaped by the router**. So load becomes bursty and skewed, and **the busiest expert dictates latency**.

2. **“MoE inference is two collective communications per layer.”**
   Every MoE layer is effectively:
   **dispatch (tokens → experts)** → **expert GEMMs** → **combine (experts → tokens)**.
   Your cost isn’t just matmul; it’s **all‑to‑all patterns + synchronization**. ([arXiv][1])

3. **“Batch size is not ‘free’ in decoding.”**
   #21 taught that decode is sequential and latency-sensitive. MoEs make this worse: you need enough tokens **per expert** to get good GEMM efficiency, but you’re constrained by KV cache + SLOs.

4. **“Expert parallelism changes *what scales*.”**
   You’re no longer just scaling tensor parallelism. You’re scaling the *placement* of experts, the *routing-induced traffic*, and the *microbatching strategy*.

5. **“Disaggregation is the new architecture knob.”**
   MegaScale‑Infer argues attention and FFNs/expert modules should be **scaled and deployed differently** (even on different GPU types), with pipelining to keep both sides busy. ([arXiv][2])

## 5) 2–3 papers to anchor #23 (with citations)

1. **MegaScale‑Infer: Serving Mixture‑of‑Experts at Scale with Disaggregated Expert Parallelism** (Zhu et al., 2025, arXiv) — disaggregates attention vs expert FFNs, introduces pipelining + specialized communication to raise utilization; reports up to **1.90×** per‑GPU decoding throughput improvements and cost reductions in deployment. ([arXiv][2])

2. **Optimizing MoE Inference Time Combining Model Deployment and Communication Scheduling (Aurora)** (Li et al., 2024, arXiv) — targets the real bottleneck: **all‑to‑all communication scheduling + expert placement**, showing sizable speedups in homogeneous and heterogeneous clusters. ([arXiv][1])

3. **Mixtral of Experts** (Jiang et al., 2024, arXiv) — the widely-referenced open MoE LLM paper; gives concrete architecture details (e.g., **top‑2 routing**) that make the serving problem tangible. ([arXiv][3])

*(Optional bonus reading if you want a deeper “systems frontier” extension later: AMoE / Asynchronous Expert Parallelism and MoEShard are strong 2025 follow-ups.)* ([arXiv][6])

   * Toggle: **expert placement** strategies (random, co-located, balanced) and observe traffic patterns.
   * Show: “dispatch bytes” and “barrier wait time”.

3. **MoE Decode Goodput Dashboard (ties directly to #21)**

   * Simulate continuous batching with request arrivals.
   * Measure TTFT/TPOT and goodput while varying:

     * microbatch size,
     * capacity factor / padding,
     * expert replication vs none.
   * “Aha”: MoE can *reduce FLOPs* but still *hurt* goodput if scheduling is naïve.

4. **Disaggregation Playground (Attention GPUs vs Expert GPUs)**

   * Two pools: attention replicas and expert nodes.
   * Let users allocate GPUs between pools; add a “ping‑pong pipeline” toggle.
   * Show utilization of each pool and end-to-end tokens/sec.
   * “Aha”: “scale attention with replication, scale experts with parallelism” becomes intuitive. ([arXiv][2])
